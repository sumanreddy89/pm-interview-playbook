# Product Critique Framework

## What This Question Tests
Tests whether you can articulate the basic principles of good design and apply them critically to evaluate a product.

## Evaluation Criteria
- Are you knowledgeable about design principles?
- Do you use design principles when evaluating products (not just personal opinion)?
- Is your answer structured?
- Are you articulate?

---

## The Framework

### Step 1: State Your Design Principles
Before critiquing, name the 3–4 principles you'll use to evaluate the product. This shows you have a framework, not just opinions.

**Common design principles to draw from:**
- **Understandable**: Users can immediately grasp what the product does and how to use it
- **Useful**: The product solves a real, meaningful problem
- **Usable**: The interface is intuitive and frictionless
- **Efficient**: Users can complete their goals quickly and with minimal effort
- **Delightful**: The product creates positive emotional responses
- **Communicative**: The product gives users clear, helpful feedback
- **Accessible**: Usable by people with a range of abilities
- **Consistent**: Behaves predictably across the product

### Step 2: Describe the Product
- What does it do?
- What problem does it solve for users?
- Who are the primary users?

### Step 3: Evaluate Against Your Principles
For each design principle, give a specific example of something the product does well or poorly.
- Be specific: name specific features, flows, or interactions
- Balance: note both what works and what doesn't

### Step 4: Recommend Improvements
Based on your evaluation, propose 2–3 concrete improvements:
- Ground each in a design principle
- Explain the user problem it solves
- Keep improvements realistic

### Step 5: Summarize
Briefly summarize: Is the product well-designed overall? What is its greatest strength and most important improvement opportunity?

---

## Alternative Framing: Compare Two Products

**When asked "Give an example of a good and not-so-good product":**
1. Pick two products that do similar things
2. Name the one design principle where the better product truly excels
3. Build a comparison table across 3–4 design dimensions
4. Conclude with possible improvements to the better product

---

## Common Question Examples
- "Critique the design of [product]."
- "Give me an example of a good and a bad product design."
- "What would you improve about [popular app]?"
- "Compare the UX of [Product A] vs. [Product B]."

## Coaching Tips
- Leading with design principles separates you from candidates who just list opinions
- The best critiques are specific — "the onboarding flow asks for too many permissions upfront" beats "the UX could be better"
- Improvement recommendations should feel empathetic — start from the user's frustration, not from what looks cool
- Don't only criticize — showing what a product does *well* demonstrates balanced judgment
