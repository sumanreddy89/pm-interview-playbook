# Design a New Product Framework

## What This Question Tests
Tests your ability to take a product from ideation to a launchable concept — covering business motivation, audience identification, problem prioritization, and solution design.

## Evaluation Criteria
- **Motivation**: Do you understand *why* the company wants to build this?
- **Segments**: Can you identify and prioritize the right target audience?
- **Problems**: Do you identify and prioritize the right problems for that segment?
- **Solution**: Are your solutions creative and well-prioritized?
- **Communication**: Structured, succinct, adaptive to interviewer input?

---

## The Framework (6 Steps)

### Step 1: Clarify
Ask questions before diving in — clarify assumptions, narrow scope:
- Who is making the product — existing company or startup?
- What is the expected launch timeline?
- Are there budget or resource constraints?
- Is there a specific platform (mobile/web/hardware)?

### Step 2: Motivation
Explain *why* the company would want to build this product. Connect it to:
- The company's mission
- Their existing product portfolio
- Market trends or competitive threats

### Step 3: Segmentation
List possible target audiences, then prioritize one with clear reasoning.
Good segmentation criteria: size of the segment, intensity of the pain, willingness to pay, strategic fit for the company.
> Tip: Don't just list demographics — describe behavioral or attitudinal attributes that differentiate segments.

### Step 4: Pain Points
For your prioritized segment, list 3–5 meaningful pain points. Then prioritize one (or a theme) using a clear rationale:
- Which pain is most severe/frequent?
- Which pain, if solved, would have the biggest impact on the business goal?

### Step 5: Solutions
Ideate 3+ solutions for the prioritized pain point. Then select one using clear criteria:
- Feasibility (technical, timeline, resources)
- Impact on the pain point
- Differentiation from existing alternatives
- Alignment with company mission

Describe the selected solution in enough detail that the interviewer can visualize it.

### Step 6: Risks
Identify 2–3 meaningful risks (not just edge cases):
- Technical feasibility risks
- Adoption risks (will users actually use it?)
- Business/competitive risks
- Regulatory/ethical risks

## Common Question Examples
- "Design a product for elderly users to manage medications."
- "Design a fridge for the office."
- "Design a Google product for college students."
- "How would you build a product to help remote teams collaborate better?"
- "Design the next big feature for [existing product]."

## Coaching Tips
- The best answers show *why* you're choosing each segment/pain/solution, not just *what*
- Avoid generic personas ("millennials who love coffee") — be specific and insightful
- Solutions should feel creative but grounded — explain the mechanism of value
- Risks signal maturity; don't skip this step even if time is short
