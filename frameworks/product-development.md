# Product Development (Trade-off Decision) Framework

## What This Question Tests
Tests how you evaluate and decide between two product development options when there are meaningful trade-offs — strategic, user-experience, technical, and business.

## Evaluation Criteria
- Do you ask clarifying questions?
- Do you have a structured approach to comparing options?
- Do you consider multiple data points (users, business, product, competition)?
- Clear and succinct communication?

---

## The Framework (7 Steps)

### Step 1: Clarify
Ask questions to understand any ambiguities:
- What is the timeline for this decision?
- Are there budget or resource constraints?
- Is one option already in progress?

### Step 2: Context
Set the business context:
- **Company**: Mission, strategy, current performance
- **Competition**: How does the company compare to competitors?
- **Product 1**: What does it do? Who are the users? What problem does it solve?
- **Product 2**: Same analysis — and how does it relate to Product 1?

### Step 3: Goals
Infer the business goals behind this decision. What is the company trying to achieve? (e.g., cross-sell, retention, efficiency, market expansion)

### Step 4: Metrics
Define metrics that would measure success for each goal:
- User metrics (DAU, MAU, session length, retention)
- Business metrics (revenue, margin, CAC)
- Product metrics (adoption rate, feature usage, completion rate)

### Step 5: Compare (Pros/Cons Across Dimensions)
Compare the two options across:

| Dimension | Option A | Option B |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Company alignment** | Does it fit mission/strategy? | Same |
| **User experience** | How does it impact users? | Same |
| **Product complexity** | Development complexity, maintenance burden | Same |
| **Business goals** | How does it advance the defined goals? | Same |
| **Metrics impact** | Expected effect on key metrics | Same |
| **Competitive response** | How will competitors react? | Same |

### Step 6: Risks
Identify risks of each option:
- Regulatory / legal
- Cannibalization of existing revenue
- User preference / adoption risk
- Demand/supply side imbalances

### Step 7: Recommendation
Synthesize the comparison into a clear recommendation:
- Which option has stronger positive vs. negative consequences?
- Is the risk level acceptable?
- What conditions would need to be true for the other option to be better?

---

## Example
**"Should Uber merge the Rides and Eats apps?"**

- **Context**: Uber's mission is to help people go anywhere and get anything; Uber Eats was less popular in 2019
- **Goals**: Cross-sell, increase Uber Eats orders, retain Eats users, maximize driver efficiency, grow revenue
- **Comparison**: Merged app = more convenient, cross-sell opportunity, competitive advantage vs. DoorDash; Risk = Eats-only users may resist
- **Recommendation**: Merge — strategic alignment is strong, user convenience improves, competitive advantage is real, risks are manageable

---

## Common Question Examples
- "Should we merge Product A and Product B into one app?"
- "Should we build Feature X or invest in Platform Y?"
- "How would you decide between a native app vs. a web app?"
- "We can either [improve core experience] or [launch a new product line]. What do you do?"

## Coaching Tips
- The context section is where you show business acumen — don't rush past it
- The comparison table should show genuine trade-offs, not just list positives for one side
- Risks are often what tip the decision — don't treat them as an afterthought
- A strong recommendation ties back clearly to the business goals you defined
